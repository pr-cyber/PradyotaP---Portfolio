Baja SAE
Competition Chassis

Designed, analyzed, and fabricated UT Austin's inaugural Baja SAE competition chassis from scratch — owning the full system from first sketch to final welded frame. 12 design iterations, ANSYS and MATLAB FEA, and a chassis that came in ~50% smaller than the initial concept while meeting Factor of Safety ≥ 2 across all load cases.

01

The Problem

UT Austin's Baja SAE team was building their first competition vehicle from zero — no legacy design to reference, no historical data, no existing team infrastructure. Every decision had to be justified from engineering fundamentals rather than prior practice.

The chassis is the most critical system on the vehicle. It must simultaneously protect the driver, interface with every other subsystem, survive unpredictable off-road impacts, and comply with SAE's strict ruleset. Getting it wrong doesn't just lose points — it fails safety.

The additional challenge: the initial concept was significantly oversized. The team needed to hit competitive mass targets without sacrificing structural integrity or rule compliance.

Engineering Challenge

"Design a first-principles competition chassis with no reference baseline — then iterate aggressively to cut mass while keeping every load case above a Factor of Safety of 2."

SolidWorks ANSYS (FEA) MATLAB Welding SAE Rulebook Structural Analysis
02

Design Process

1

Requirements & Rulebook Analysis

Translated SAE Baja rulebook requirements into structural design constraints. Defined minimum tube sizing, required rollover protection geometry, driver egress clearances, and interface points for powertrain, suspension, and steering.

2

Initial Concept Modeling (SolidWorks)

Built the first full chassis model in SolidWorks. Established the primary tube layout, node geometry, and key dimensions. Used parametric modeling so dimensions could be efficiently updated across iterations without rebuilding from scratch.

3

FEA Load Case Analysis (ANSYS + MATLAB)

Ran structural FEA in ANSYS across all critical load cases: frontal impact, rollover, torsion, side loading, and combined-load scenarios. Used MATLAB to automate parametric sweeps across tube wall thickness, diameter, and node angles to evaluate mass vs. FoS trade-offs across hundreds of configurations.

4

12 Iterations — Converging on Optimal Design

Each iteration used FEA results to drive targeted geometry changes. The binding constraint that emerged was torsional rigidity — not peak stress. Redesigning node geometry to optimize torsional stiffness-to-mass drove the largest reductions. The final chassis was ~50% smaller than the initial concept.

5

Fabrication & Manufacturing Process Development

Welded and assembled multiple frames. Developed repeatable jig setups and welding sequences to ensure dimensional accuracy across builds. Established manufacturing standards for the new team so future builds could be executed consistently without depending on a single person's experience.

Key Results & Insights

📐

~50% size reduction from initial concept to final design, achieved through 12 full FEA-driven design iterations.

✓

Factor of Safety ≥ 2 maintained across all load cases — frontal impact, rollover, side load, torsion, and combined scenarios.

🔍

Torsional rigidity — not peak stress — was the binding constraint. This was only visible through parametric FEA sweeps. Redesigning node geometry to optimize torsional stiffness-per-unit-mass unlocked the largest mass savings.

⚙

Established repeatable manufacturing processes for the team: jig setups, weld sequences, and tube preparation standards that future builds can follow without depending on institutional knowledge from a single person.

📋

Full SAE rulebook compliance achieved, including rollover protection geometry, driver egress clearances, and minimum tube sizing requirements.
