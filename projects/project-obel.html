Optothermal Bubble
Etch Lithography

A 1.5-year experimental research program developing Optothermal Bubble Etch Lithography (OBEL) — a sustainable, maskless nanomanufacturing technique using laser-induced microbubbles on gold thin films. Achieved sub-micron feature resolution (~450 nm) at ultralow etchant concentrations. Co-authored and presented at SPIE's Optical Trapping & Micromanipulation XXII.

The Problem

Conventional photolithography for nanoscale device fabrication requires expensive photomasks, high chemical consumption, vacuum environments, and produces significant thermal damage to substrates. These constraints make nanofabrication inaccessible, chemically wasteful, and poorly suited to on-demand or low-volume precision work.

A maskless, spatially selective alternative would dramatically reduce material cost, chemical waste, and process complexity — while enabling fabrication patterns that would be difficult or impossible with conventional mask-based lithography.

The research question: can laser-induced microbubble formation on gold thin films enable localized, high-precision etching at the sub-micron scale with minimal chemical and thermal damage?

Research Question

"Can solutal Marangoni convection around a laser-induced microbubble enable sub-micron feature resolution without the masks, chemicals, and thermal damage of conventional lithography?"

02

Experimental Approach

1

Laser-Induced Microbubble Formation

Focused a laser on a gold thin film submerged in etchant solution. Localized heating creates a microbubble at the film surface. The bubble acts as a spatially selective reaction site — etchant is concentrated at the bubble boundary, etching the gold film in a defined location.

2

Marangoni Convection for Byproduct Removal

Solutal Marangoni convection — driven by concentration gradients at the bubble surface — actively removes reaction byproducts and clears the etch site. This produces sharply defined feature edges rather than the diffuse, smeared boundaries seen without convective removal.

3

Parameter Optimization (200+ Experiments)

Systematically varied laser power, etchant concentration, scan speed, and bubble residence time. Collected 1,000+ data points via LabVIEW and NIS imaging software. Identified the narrow parameter window that produces sub-micron features without thermal damage to surrounding film.

4

Feature Characterization & Validation

Characterized resulting features using optical and electron microscopy. Measured feature widths, edge definition, and etch depth. Achieved ~450 nm feature resolution at etchant concentrations far below those required by conventional etching processes.

Results & Publication

🔬

~450 nm feature resolution achieved — sub-micron precision at etchant concentrations far below those required by conventional etch processes.

⚗

Ultralow etchant concentrations required, demonstrating an environmentally sustainable alternative to chemical-intensive conventional nanolithography.

📊

Laser power and etchant concentration interact nonlinearly. A narrow operating window produces clean sub-micron features while avoiding thermal damage — making systematic parameter optimization essential.

📄

Co-authored and presented at Optical Trapping and Optical Micromanipulation XXII (SPIE). DOI: 10.1117/12.3061570 ↗
