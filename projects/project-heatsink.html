Heat Sink
Geometry Optimizer

Built a Python tool that automates the thermal performance evaluation of rectangular fin heat sinks by sweeping fin geometry parameters — fin height, fin thickness, fin spacing, and base thickness — across both forced and natural convection heat transfer models. Performance surfaces are visualized with Matplotlib, and Pareto-optimal designs are extracted across thermal resistance and total mass, surfacing high-performance configurations invisible to manual hand-calculation.

The Problem

Heat sink design is a multi-variable optimization problem with strong parameter interactions. Increasing fin height increases surface area but also increases fin resistance; decreasing fin spacing enhances convection but eventually causes boundary layer merging that degrades heat transfer; thicker fins add mass and material cost while only marginally reducing conduction resistance. No single parameter can be tuned in isolation.

Conventional heat sink selection relies on manufacturer catalogs and simplified correlations applied to one geometry at a time. This approach misses the interaction effects between geometry, airflow conditions, and thermal boundary conditions — and produces designs that are often significantly over- or under-designed for their actual application.

The goal: build a Python tool that numerically evaluates the full geometric parameter space, applies convection correlations for both forced and natural flow, identifies the thermal resistance vs. mass Pareto front, and outputs ranked candidate geometries for any given thermal load and airflow condition.

Core Insight

"Fin geometry interactions are nonlinear and multi-directional. Only a full parameter sweep reveals the true Pareto boundary between thermal resistance and heat sink mass."

Engineering Approach

1

Fin Geometry Parameterization

Defined the heat sink geometry by four independent parameters: fin height H, fin thickness t, fin-to-fin spacing s, and base plate thickness t_b. Number of fins was computed from base width and fin pitch. Total surface area, fin efficiency (using analytical fin theory), and overall surface efficiency were derived from geometry for each candidate.

2

Convection Modeling — Forced & Natural

For forced convection, applied channel flow Nusselt number correlations (Dittus–Boelter and developing-flow corrections) as a function of fin channel hydraulic diameter, airflow velocity, and fluid properties. For natural convection, applied vertical plate and channel correlations. Both models returned heat transfer coefficient h as a function of geometry and operating conditions.

3

Thermal Resistance Calculation

Computed total thermal resistance R_th = R_conduction + R_convection for each geometry candidate. R_conduction computed from base thickness and material conductivity. R_convection computed from h, total effective surface area, and overall surface efficiency. This gave a single performance metric (°C/W) comparable across all candidate geometries.

4

Full Parameter Sweep & Pareto Analysis

Swept all four geometric parameters across defined ranges and evaluated thermal resistance and total mass for each combination — generating thousands of candidates per operating condition. Applied Pareto analysis to identify designs that simultaneously minimize both thermal resistance and heat sink mass, extracting the efficiency frontier of the design space.

5

Performance Surface Visualization

Generated 3D surface plots and 2D contour maps of thermal resistance as a function of fin height and spacing at fixed thickness values, revealing nonlinear interaction regions. Plotted Pareto fronts per airflow condition with candidate annotations for fin count, height, and spacing to enable direct engineering selection.

Results & Key Insights

🌡

Nonlinear thermal resistance minimum identified at intermediate fin heights — taller fins past a threshold increased convective resistance rather than reducing it, due to fin efficiency degradation and boundary layer effects.

📊

Optimal fin spacing is flow-regime dependent: forced convection favored tighter spacing (more fins, higher surface area); natural convection favored wider spacing (improved buoyancy-driven airflow). Designs optimized for one regime performed poorly in the other.

🏆

Pareto analysis exposed a 30–40% mass reduction opportunity on conventional catalog heat sinks — designs at the Pareto frontier achieved equivalent thermal resistance at significantly lower mass than typical commercially available solutions for comparable power dissipation targets.

🔁

Fully reusable tool: input any heat load (W), base dimensions, material (Al 6061, Cu, Al 1100), and airflow condition — the tool regenerates the full sweep, Pareto front, and ranked candidate list automatically.
