Urban
Resilience Model

Developed an Agent-Based Model (ABM) in Python to simulate the complex, emergent dynamics between housing availability, economic instability, and homelessness in Seattle, WA. The model stress-tests urban resilience against "Black Swan" events — natural disasters, economic recessions, and sudden demographic shifts — and serves as a customizable policy sandbox for evaluating which interventions most effectively reduce long-term homelessness.

The Problem

Homelessness in cities like Seattle is not a static, linear problem — it's a dynamic system with nonlinear thresholds. Small changes in income, housing supply, or unemployment can push individual citizens past a point of no return, while large aggregate interventions sometimes produce surprisingly small effects. Traditional policy analysis based on macro-level statistics misses these threshold dynamics entirely.

Agent-Based Modeling addresses this gap by simulating individual citizens ("agents") with unique, realistic attributes — income progression, age-based savings behavior, probabilistic unemployment risk, and housing purchase decisions. Emergent macro-level patterns (homelessness rates, housing market tightness) arise from thousands of individual agent decisions rather than being imposed by top-down equations.

The goal: build a validated ABM for Seattle's housing market that can stress-test "what if" scenarios, identify critical economic thresholds, and rank policy interventions by their long-run impact on homelessness outcomes.

Key Insight

"Reducing unemployment rates via job market expansion outperformed housing supply expansion and direct income transfers in long-term homelessness reduction — a counterintuitive result that only emerged through agent-level simulation."

Model Architecture

1

Agent Design — Buyer/Seller Citizens

Each agent is initialized with a random age (20–70), income ($39k–$170k drawn from Seattle income distributions), and savings proportional to age × income × 0.1 (the 20% savings rule scaled by working years). Agents begin as buyers (93.2%) or sellers (7.8%) matching real Seattle housing market turnover rates. Unemployment probability is calibrated to historical unemployment curves via quadratic regression on real-world data.

2

Economic Data Calibration

Synthesized real-world datasets from Gusto (income distributions), Mathworks (housing price trajectories), and historical Seattle unemployment data spanning 2000–2022. Applied quadratic polynomial regression to extract time-varying functions for median income, unemployment rate, and house price — used to update agent parameters each simulation year rather than using fixed values.

3

Housing Market Mechanics

Each simulation step, buyer agents scan the available seller pool for properties within their budget. Successful transactions remove the seller from the pool and transfer the house. Agents who lose housing (unemployment, aging out, budget depletion) become sellers. Agents without housing face a probabilistic homelessness trigger (45–80% depending on housing supply tightness). Population grows at Seattle's historical 1.024% annual rate to maintain demographic realism.

4

Stochastic "Black Swan" Shock — The 20-Year Pulse

Based on historical frequency data (4–5 major disruptions per century), the model injects a stochastic shock at simulation year 20 (2042). Three shock types were implemented: (1) Natural Disaster — housing prices drop $100k, income falls, population declines 1%/year as the area becomes less desirable; (2) Economic Recession — unemployment rises, house prices spike, income falls simultaneously; (3) Migrant Influx — population grows 3%/year, housing demand surges, wages compress from labor market competition. All scenarios implement exponential recovery curves calibrated to historical post-disaster economic data.

5

Policy Sensitivity Analysis

Systematically varied unemployment rate, housing supply, and income floor parameters to rank intervention types by their impact on long-run homelessness rates across all three Black Swan scenarios. Compared job creation programs, housing supply expansion, and direct income transfer interventions using the model as a controlled sandbox — holding all other parameters constant while varying the intervention magnitude.

Key Findings & Insights

📊

Job creation dominated all other interventions in long-term homelessness reduction across all three Black Swan scenarios. Reducing unemployment rates outperformed direct housing supply expansion and income transfer programs by a significant margin in every simulation run.

⚡

Threshold dynamics identified: a savings depletion "Abyss" threshold exists below which agents cannot recover without external intervention. Once agents cross this line — triggered by unemployment, aging, or disaster — they enter a self-reinforcing homelessness loop even as the economy recovers around them.

🌊

Migrant Influx was the most damaging Black Swan scenario for existing residents — housing prices jumped fastest (to $600k), income compression from labor market competition was most severe, and the unemployed homeless probability rose to 80% due to reduced housing availability.

🔍

Natural Disaster caused the fastest short-term housing instability but the least permanent damage — property value drops and population decline actually reduced housing competition for surviving residents over the long run.

📐

Computational scalability documented: 200 high-fidelity agents on standard hardware. Model architecture is linearly scalable to Seattle's full population (~750k) via cloud-based HPC — with modular code structure designed for future "Quality of Housing" (gentrification/decay) extensions.
