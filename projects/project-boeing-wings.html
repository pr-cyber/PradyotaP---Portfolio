The Role

As a Design Engineering Intern on the 737 Wings Structures team, the focus shifts from the factory floor to the drawing board. Where my 2025 internship was about understanding what happens when design meets production, this role is about shaping the design itself — working on primary structural components that carry flight loads, resist fatigue, and meet FAA certification requirements.

The wings team is responsible for one of the most structurally complex and aerodynamically critical systems on the aircraft. Primary structures — spars, ribs, skin panels, winglets, trailing edge assemblies, and leading edge slats — must simultaneously minimize weight, survive the full aircraft design life, and be manufacturable at production scale.

This role builds directly on ANSYS FEA, SolidWorks, and CATIA experience from coursework and Baja SAE, applied now to certification-grade structural analysis in an aerospace context.

Wing Structure Focus Areas

The 737 wing is a primary load-bearing structure carrying all flight, ground, and gust loads between the fuselage and engines. The Wings Structures team works across several distinct zones, each with its own structural character and design challenges.

🛩
Winglets
Upswept wingtip extensions that reduce induced drag and improve fuel efficiency. Structurally complex — must transfer bending and torsion loads while resisting fatigue at the wing tip attachment.
⬇
Trailing Edge
Houses the flap and aileron systems that control lift and roll. Complex multi-element structures must be aerodynamically precise and survive repeated actuation loads across the aircraft's design life.
⬆
Leading Edge
Slats, Krueger flaps, and thermal anti-icing structures. Critical for low-speed performance and icing certification. Must be lightweight, reliable, and manufacturable within extremely tight tolerances.
